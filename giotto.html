Giotto: Internet of Things Privacy

As ubiquitous sensing and computing is becoming more and more common in today's world, it is increasingly more difficult to keep tabs on what information is being collected around you. The Giotto Internet of Things (IoT) Expedition is a project focused on advancing the state of the art for privacy for the Internet of Things. 

The main goal of Giotto is to grant end-users awareness of all nearby IoT devices which are actively collecting data about them. By the time I joined this project, the architecture for a system that aggregates and stores super-sensor information was already built. This system, called Building Depot, allows for rich sensor data to be access and shared. With this in place, our team was positioned to create a layer of abstraction between this raw data and the end users, keeping their privacy in mind along the way.

In order to gauge how end users view and value their privacy in the context of ubiquitous applications, our team ideated a plethora of potential IoT use cases. We used these along with student surveys to determine the important insight that users think IoT applications are extremely interesting and helpful, but are timid to provide personal data in order to fuel said applications.

We additionally discovered that people are comfortable sharing varying levels of personal information to different groups of people. This insight led us to the idea of creating a tier-based API to handle how sensor-collected information is shared. Each of these Tiers has access to a different level of granularity when it comes to sharing data. Based on how each individual buckets others into these tiers, they have complete control over who can see/access certain sensory-driven information about them.

The main use case that we decided to focus on to convey this tier-based system was location, which I prototyped in order to validate our assumptions of user's attitudes towards their privacy.
